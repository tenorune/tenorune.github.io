Removal orders accumulate in immigration files like sediment. A person can carry one for thirty years without consequence, having long since built a life unrecognized by the document. Through early 2026, thirteen thousand of those long-dormant orders were activated under the second Trump administration's third-country deportation framework — sending people not to their country of origin, but to whatever third country could be persuaded or coerced to receive them.

The legal authority is contested but technically existent. What is genuinely new is the willingness to use it at scale, combined with the choice of destination. Previous administrations encountered the same dormant orders and the same statutory tools and concluded, generally without articulating the reasoning, that the cost — in destabilized lives, in deaths in unfamiliar countries, in international relationship damage — was not worth the enforcement gain. That cost-benefit conclusion has been reversed.

The thirteen-thousand number is itself a partial count. Each entry corresponds to a person whose case was opened decades or generations ago and reopened in 2025-26 specifically to enable a removal that would not have happened in any prior decade.
