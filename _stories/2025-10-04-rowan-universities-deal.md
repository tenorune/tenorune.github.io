When the Trump administration circulated a draft "compact" inviting universities to accept policy concessions in exchange for continued federal research funding, much of its language and many of its specific demands came not from the Department of Education but from a memo authored at the behest of Apollo Global Management chief executive Marc Rowan. The Times documented multiple provisions whose wording was lifted, in some cases verbatim, from the Rowan-circulated document into the federal text. Rowan is among the most active private donors in the Republican policy-influence network around higher education.

The pattern is familiar enough — well-resourced private interests propose; an administration ratifies — but the explicitness of the lift is unusual. A federal proposal is not normally a clean copy-paste of a single donor's draft. The story is less about Rowan personally than about the granularity with which billionaire policy memos can become federal documents when an administration is amenable, and how thin the membrane is, at present, between the two.
