U.S. District Judge Joseph Goodwin, sitting in the Southern District of West Virginia, issued a ruling in February 2026 on the treatment of immigration detainees held in his jurisdiction. The line that closed the ruling — and that drew it into the public conversation — was: "This is not what civil enforcement looks like in a humane system of government under law." Politico reporter Kyle Cheney, who has been tracking the federal-court output of the second Trump administration, surfaced the opinion as one of a small set of judicial documents that articulate, on the record, what is and is not normal in U.S. immigration detention.

The line is a judicial qualifier, not a finding of fact about a particular detainee. Its weight comes from being delivered in an opinion — a document that goes into the federal record and is citeable by other judges — rather than in commentary or a press release. Goodwin is a Clinton appointee with two decades on the bench. The phrase reads less as advocacy than as definition: the judge is reserving the descriptor "humane" for a system the one in front of him is not.
